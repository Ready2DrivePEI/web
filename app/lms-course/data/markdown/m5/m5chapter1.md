# Cyclists and the Road — Equal Rights, Shared Space

Bicycle use keeps growing as more people ride for transportation, exercise, and saving energy. Under PEI law, cyclists must obey the same rules of the road as motor vehicles — and they are entitled to an equal share of the road.

This matters to you whether you ride a bicycle yourself or only drive a car. Bicycle riders, regardless of age, must know and follow traffic regulations. In most cases, these are the same rules that apply to motor vehicle drivers.

# What Motorists Must Know About Cyclists

As a driver, understanding how cyclists behave — and how vulnerable they are — is essential to preventing collisions.

When sharing the road with cyclists:

- Many children on bicycles are very young and lack the training for safe cycling. Be especially alert for small children on oversized bikes — they are more likely to make sudden, erratic moves.
- When passing a cyclist, go around them the same way you would pass any other vehicle. Give them space.
- Before making a right turn, watch for cyclists who may pull up alongside your vehicle. Check your blind spots.
- Do not pull up beside a cyclist and then turn right directly in front of them — this cuts them off and can cause a serious crash.
- When pulling away from the curb, always check for cyclists who may be trying to pass you.
- When parked at the curb, always check for cyclists before opening your vehicle door. It is the driver's responsibility not to open the door into oncoming traffic.
- Do not follow too closely behind cyclists — they have no brake lights to warn you when they are stopping.
- Cyclists are entitled to make left turns the same way motorists do. On multi-lane roads, give them extra consideration since they are fully exposed.
- Cyclists are entitled to ride at least one metre from the curb and may ride even further out to avoid drainage grates, potholes, debris, loose gravel, wet surfaces, rutted pavement, or dogs. Be aware of road conditions that may force a cyclist to move further into the lane.

> ⚠️ WARNING: Do Not Honk at Cyclists
> Blowing your horn when passing a cyclist may frighten them and cause them to swerve into your path. If you must use the horn, tap it quickly and lightly while you are still some distance away.

# Rules of the Road for Cyclists

Now that you understand how drivers should behave around cyclists, here are the rules cyclists themselves must follow on PEI roads.

**DO:**
- Keep both hands on the handlebars at all times, except when making a hand signal.
- Keep both feet on the pedals.
- Ride in single file, except when overtaking and passing another bicycle.
- Ride on the right side of the roadway, as close as safely possible to the right curb or edge of the road.
- Use a bicycle path adjacent to a street or highway if one exists.
- Ride on the regular seat of the bicycle.
- Carry parcels in bicycle carriers so they do not interfere with your control of the bike.

**DO NOT:**
- Ride on a sidewalk.
- Ride on a highway where signs prohibit bicycles.
- Ride the wrong way on a one-way street — there are no traffic signs in place for wrong-way riders.
- Carry more people than the bicycle was designed for.
- Hold onto or attach the bicycle to any other moving vehicle.

### How to Prevent Cyclist Collisions

- Always stop and check for traffic when coming out of a lane or driveway.
- Be alert for cars pulling out from driveways, lanes, or parking spaces.
- At intersections, watch for cars turning right — they may squeeze you against the curb.
- Yield the right-of-way to pedestrians.

Q: Can a cyclist ride on the sidewalk if the road feels unsafe?
A: No. Cyclists are not allowed to ride on sidewalks under PEI law, regardless of how the road feels.

# Required Bicycle Equipment

Riding safely means more than just following traffic rules — your bicycle itself must be properly equipped, especially for night riding.

**Brakes**: Your bike must have effective brakes. You should be able to make the braked wheels skid on dry, level, clean pavement.

**Night riding equipment — your bike must have:**

| Equipment | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Front lamp | Visible from 150 metres (500 feet) |
| Rear red reflector | Visible from 90 metres (300 feet) |
| Bell or signalling device | Audible from at least 30 metres (100 feet) |

Always wear light-coloured or reflective clothing when riding at night.

# E-Bikes in PEI

E-bikes look similar to bicycles but are classified very differently under PEI law.

Under the Highway Traffic Act, E-bikes are considered limited-speed motorcycles. They fall under the same rules as mopeds. This means you need a valid driver's licence to operate an E-bike on Prince Edward Island.

> ⚠️ WARNING: E-Bikes Are Not Regular Bicycles
> Even though E-bikes resemble bicycles, they are legally classified as mopeds. Riding one without a valid driver's licence is illegal.

Q: Can I ride an E-bike with just a bicycle helmet and no licence?
A: No. E-bikes are classified as mopeds under the Highway Traffic Act. A valid driver's licence is required.

# Bicycle Helmets — Why and How They Protect You

Beyond proper equipment and road rules, protecting your head is the single most critical safety measure when cycling.

Under the Bicycle Helmet Regulations, everyone who operates a bicycle must wear an approved safety helmet. A brain injury from a cycling crash can change your life permanently — it can affect your ability to read, speak, move, or even feed yourself.

A bicycle helmet has a hard plastic shell on the outside and crushable foam on the inside. When your head hits the ground, the foam absorbs the impact by compressing, which cushions the blow and protects your brain. The shell makes the helmet skid on the surface so your neck doesn't get jerked, and it holds the foam together so it doesn't split apart between impacts.

If the chin strap is not tight, the helmet can slip to the side or back, leaving your bare head to hit the road.

### Key Concepts

- **Choosing a helmet**: Look for a CPSC certification sticker inside. That confirms the helmet meets safety standards. You don't need to spend a lot — just make sure it fits properly and you'll actually wear it.
- **After a crash**: Replace the helmet. A bicycle helmet is only good for one crash. The foam is designed to crush once.
- **In-line skating**: A bike helmet can be used for in-line skating, but skateboarders need a different type of helmet.

---

# Fitting a Bicycle Helmet — The Five-Step Test

A helmet that doesn't fit correctly can fail to protect you, even if it's the best helmet on the market. Allow up to half an hour to get the fit right.

**Position**: Place the helmet on your head so it sits evenly between your ears and rests low on your forehead — about one to two finger widths above your eyebrows.

**Pads**: Insert the foam pads inside the helmet until it feels snug but comfortable. Helmets usually come with multiple pad sizes for a custom fit.

**Straps**: Tighten the chin strap as snugly as possible. Adjust the junction of the front and back straps so it sits just under each ear.

### How It Works — The Five-Step Fit Test

| Step | Test | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Gently lift the front of the helmet up and back | Helmet moves back, uncovering the forehead | Tighten the front strap. Adjust rear padding. Make sure chin strap is snug. If it still moves, the helmet may be too big. |
| 2 | Gently lift the back of the helmet up and forward | Helmet slides forward over the eyes | Tighten the back strap. Adjust front padding. Ensure chin strap is snug. |
| 3 | Rock the helmet side to side; shake your head "no" hard | Helmet slips from side to side | Check side padding and make sure straps are evenly adjusted. |
| 4 | Open your mouth as wide as possible without moving your head | Helmet does not pull down when you open your mouth | Tighten chin strap. Make sure strap junction is under each ear. |
| 5 | Check the front edge of the helmet | Helmet does not cover the forehead | Position helmet 1–2 finger widths above eyebrows. Tighten loose straps. |

After adjusting, have someone else repeat the five-step test on you while you hold your head still. The helmet should pass all five steps.

**Summary**
Cyclists have the same rights and responsibilities on PEI roads as motor vehicle drivers. Motorists must give cyclists space, check blind spots before turning or opening doors, and never honk while passing. Cyclists must ride on the right, obey traffic rules, use proper lights and reflectors at night, and always wear an approved helmet. E-bikes are classified as mopeds and require a valid driver's licence. A properly fitted helmet — verified by the five-step fit test — is your last line of defence in a crash.
