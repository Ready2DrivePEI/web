# Right-of-Way, Intersections, Pedestrians, and Core Legal Duties

## Lesson 1: Driver Responsibility and Occupant Safety

### Collisions, Restraints, and the Law

Responsible drivers obey traffic regulations and drive carefully so they do not harm people or damage property. Collisions can happen even when you are careful. Unrestrained people can strike each other, hit the inside of the vehicle, or be thrown out. Severe injury or death often results.

Seat belts and approved child restraints help keep everyone in place. They also help in everyday driving when you swerve or stop suddenly to avoid a crash. Fewer than one percent of injury-producing collisions involve fire or submersion. In those situations, a seat belt greatly improves the chance you stay conscious so you can get out.

Everyone age 16 and older must wear a seat belt when a belt is available at their seating position. The driver must make sure every passenger under 16 wears a seat belt when a belt is available. Young children must use an approved child restraint suited to them.

### Infants, Toddlers, and Booster Seats

Infants from birth weight to 10 kg (22 lb), with a minimum age of one year, must travel rear-facing in a child restraint that meets CMVSS Standard 213.1 and PEI Seat Belt Regulations. That may be a portable infant carrier or a convertible seat. Current regulations state infants from birth weight to 10 kg (22 lb) must travel rear-facing.

Toddlers from 10 to 18 kg (22 to 40 lb) must use a child restraint seat that meets the Highway Traffic Act or CMVSS. It can be the same convertible seat or a toddler-only seat. When a toddler rides in a vehicle other than a parent's or legal guardian's, a lap belt may be used. Forward-facing seats must use a tether strap with a proper anchor.

Booster seats are required for children who are too big for a child car seat but too small to be protected by a seat belt. That includes preschool to primary-grade children weighing between 18 and 36 kg (40 to 80 lb), with standing height less than 145 cm (four feet nine inches), or under age ten. All booster seats must be used with the lap and shoulder belt for children who have outgrown a forward-facing child restraint. Use the booster in the back seat with the lap belt snug over the hips, not the stomach. Children under 12 should ride in the back.

### Key Concepts

*   **Lap and shoulder belts**: Lap belts should be snug and low on the hips. Never use a shoulder belt without a lap belt. Leave about a fist's width between chest and shoulder belt. Shoulder belts are not recommended for anyone under about 140 cm (47 in) tall.
*   **Air bags**: Air bags help in frontal crashes only, not rear-end, side, or rollover crashes. They may deploy at speeds as low as about 15 km/h. Seat belts protect in all collision types. In low and moderate speeds, belts alone are enough; in high-speed crashes, bags and belts together work best.
*   **Children and air bags**: Do not sit or stand close to the dashboard. Never put a rear-facing infant seat where an air bag can strike it.
*   **Why belts matter**: Thrown-from-vehicle risk is many times higher without belts. Belts help you stay in control. Most crashes happen at 60 km/h or less and close to home. Unbelted people can injure others in the car. Never hold a child on your lap. Never put two people in one belt.

## Lesson 2: Right-of-Way at Multi-Way Stops and Entering a Road

### What the Rules of the Road Are

The Rules of the Road are laws and safe practices for moving vehicles, pedestrians, and other road users. You must know and apply them to get a licence.

### All-Way Stops

At three-way or four-way stops, if two or more vehicles enter from different directions at about the same time, the vehicle on the left yields to the vehicle on the right. Slow enough that you can stop if a vehicle from the right is so close or fast that a crash would happen if both kept going. Many of these intersections are in residential areas, so views of cars, cyclists, pedestrians, and children are often blocked. Slow to a speed where you still have full control. Entering first does not automatically give you right-of-way.

### Stopping Before You Enter

Before you enter a highway, you must come to a complete stop and not go unless you can do so safely. Stop before any marked or unmarked crosswalk. If there is no crosswalk, stop before the travelled portion of the highway you are entering.

### Rules to Remember

*   **Same time at all-way stops**: The vehicle on the left yields to the vehicle on the right.
*   **Where to stop**: Complete stop before the crosswalk, or if none, before the travelled part of the road you enter.

> 💡 NOTE: Even when rules seem to favour you, drive so you can still avoid a crash if someone else makes a mistake.

## Lesson 3: Right-of-Way When Turning Left

### Your Duties on a Left Turn

A left turn has several duties. When you turn left you must:

*   **Lane**: Be in the proper lane.
*   **Signal**: Give a visible signal of your intention to turn.
*   **Yield**: Yield the right-of-way to any vehicle or pedestrian within the intersection or so close to it that a collision could result.

## Lesson 4: Driveways, Lanes, and Alleys

### Entering from a Private Road or Drive (Rural)

If you enter or cross a highway from a private road, lane, driveway, or building in a rural area, you must stop and yield the right-of-way to traffic on the street or highway. Do not enter the highway if there is any danger of collision. Do not enter if the other driver would be forced to swerve or brake to avoid a collision.

### Entering in a 50 km/h Town or City Zone

In a restricted-speed area such as a 50 km/h (30 mph) zone in any city or town, you must stop your car before driving onto the crosswalk, check for pedestrians and other vehicles that may be approaching and yield the right-of-way to them, and not proceed until you are sure it is safe to do so. The responsibility to avoid a collision rests with you.

## Lesson 5: Right-of-Way at Stop Signs

### Why Stop Signs Exist

Stop signs are placed at intersections where extra hazards exist, such as heavy traffic or limited visibility. Start slowing soon enough that you can stop smoothly.

### Where to Stop: City or Town

In a city or town, stop your vehicle before entering a crosswalk, at a clearly marked stop line, or at the point nearest the intersecting roadways where pedestrians would normally cross.

### Where to Stop: Rural Areas

In rural areas, stop your vehicle before the edge of the roadway you are about to enter. Look carefully in both directions for the speed and distance of approaching traffic. If the road is slippery you need a larger gap before you can start up. Look twice in both directions, starting with the left (or closest lanes). Yield the right-of-way to any traffic that is close, or is travelling at such a speed, that there would be danger of collision if you proceeded.

### Full Stops, Not Rolling Slowdowns

Rolling a stop sign is illegal. Stop signs mark higher-risk intersections. Slowing rather than stopping is dangerous and leads to collisions. If you are a beginner, develop the habit of always making a full stop. If you have driven for years, you may need to correct a habit of rolling through stop signs.

### Common Mistakes

*   **Rolling through**: Treating a stop sign as "slow enough" instead of a full stop.
*   **Stop position**: Stopping beyond the legal point or blocking the crosswalk.

## Lesson 6: Emergency Vehicles and Funeral Processions

### Emergency Vehicles

Whenever an emergency vehicle, ambulance, fire department vehicle, or police car sounding a siren, horn, or warning signals approaches from any direction, you must yield the right-of-way by:

*   **Intersections**: Immediately moving clear of an intersection.
*   **Position**: Driving as closely as possible to the right-hand curb or edge of the roadway.
*   **Stop**: Stopping and remaining stopped until the emergency vehicle or vehicles have passed.

On one-way streets, pull to the right or left as appropriate.

### Funeral Processions

Drivers of vehicles in a funeral procession with lighted headlights must exercise care when coming to a red traffic light or stop sign. Only if a police officer is present and directs may you proceed through the red light or stop sign with caution. All approaching vehicles must reduce their speed to half the posted speed limit. At no time may a driver attempt to pass through a funeral procession from behind. Funeral vehicles on PEI are equipped with purple flashing lights.

> ⚠️ WARNING: Emergency vehicles need the road  
> Failing to yield can delay emergency response and cause secondary crashes. Clear the intersection, move to the curb side, and stay stopped until they pass.

## Lesson 7: Right-of-Way at Yield Signs

### What a Yield Sign Requires

Yield signs provide a flexible way to control traffic at certain locations. When approaching a yield sign, you must slow down to a speed that makes it possible for you to stop and yield the right-of-way to pedestrians and vehicles. If necessary, stop and yield the right-of-way to anyone on the roadway, then proceed with caution.

### How It Works

Think of a yield as "be ready to stop," not "never stop." Move only when gaps are truly clear.

## Lesson 8: Pedestrians, Signals, and Crosswalks

### Signalized Intersections

Both drivers and pedestrians must obey the traffic signal that controls an intersection. Whenever a pedestrian enters the crosswalk with a "walk" or green signal light, the pedestrian has the right-of-way over all vehicles. A pedestrian must not leave the curb and walk or run into the path of a vehicle that is so close that a driver cannot safely yield the right-of-way.

When the amber light or "wait" sign appears, a pedestrian must not start to cross. If a pedestrian is in the intersection when the amber light or "wait" sign appears, he or she must proceed to the nearest sidewalk. A driver must yield the right-of-way to a pedestrian and must not proceed until the pedestrian is safely across the street.

### Flashing Red Lights

When approaching an intersection controlled by a flashing red light, a driver must come to a full stop and remain stopped until it is safe to proceed. Pedestrians may enter the roadway with caution and have the right-of-way over vehicles.

### Flashing Amber

When approaching an intersection controlled by a flashing amber light, a driver must enter with caution and yield the right-of-way to any pedestrians within the crosswalks. Pedestrians should remember that drivers are not required to come to a full stop for a flashing amber light, and before entering the roadway against an amber light a pedestrian should exercise extra care.

### No Traffic Signals

Pedestrians have the right-of-way within a marked or unmarked crosswalk at an intersection with a stop sign or yield sign, and at an open intersection. Drivers must yield to a pedestrian. Pedestrians should always exercise caution when entering an intersection where drivers are not required to stop.

### Key Concepts

*   **"Walk" or green for pedestrians**: Vehicles yield and do not proceed until the pedestrian is safely across.
*   **Flashing red**: Full stop for drivers until safe; pedestrians cross with caution and have priority over vehicles.
*   **Flashing amber**: Drivers enter with caution and yield to pedestrians in crosswalks; pedestrians expect that drivers may not stop fully.

## Lesson 9: Vehicles Stopped for Pedestrians

### The No-Pass Rule

One frequent cause of pedestrian collisions is one vehicle passing another that is stopped for pedestrians. When a vehicle has stopped for a pedestrian, a driver approaching from behind must not pass the stopped vehicle.

The driver of the first vehicle approaching the crosswalk should attempt to stop a sufficient distance away to give other drivers a clear view of the crosswalk, and the driver of the oncoming vehicle must also stop. Always use extreme caution before you attempt to pass a vehicle which is stopped or slowing down at an intersection. If you are about to pass a larger vehicle, be aware of a pedestrian who may walk or run into your path. Slow down as you are about to pass. Never pass a vehicle stopped for a pedestrian.

### Care for Pedestrians Everywhere

The law requires that you exercise care for the safety of pedestrians wherever they may be crossing. In residential areas, slow down and be on alert for youngsters who may suddenly run into your path. If you see any children, or a confused or incapacitated person, slow down and be prepared to stop. Always stop for pedestrians within crosswalks and never pass a vehicle that has stopped for a pedestrian.

### White Canes and Seeing-Eye Dogs

A blind or partially blind person carries a white cane to indicate that he or she cannot see. Whenever you see a pedestrian carrying a white cane, drive with caution and be prepared to stop. Often a blind person will raise his or her cane when they are uncertain of crossing the street in safety. That is your signal to stop and allow him or her to go ahead. The same courtesy should be extended to persons led by a seeing-eye dog.

### Restrictions

*   **Passing**: You must not pass a vehicle that has stopped for a pedestrian.
*   **Passing near intersections**: Use extreme caution before passing any vehicle stopped or slowing at an intersection; the other driver may see hazards you cannot.

## Lesson 10: Intersection and Crossing Judgment

### A Simple Decision Flow

Use the same thinking at every intersection or crossing. Identify what controls the location: stop sign, yield, lights, or no signal. Stop where the law requires. Scan for vehicles, pedestrians, and cyclists, including hidden approaches. Decide who has right-of-way under the rules you have learned. Move only when you can do so safely and without forcing others to brake or swerve.

### Summary

Driver responsibility starts with protecting yourself and passengers through seat belts and correct child restraints. Right-of-way rules at stops, yields, left turns, and private entrances all come back to yielding when others are close enough that moving would risk a crash. Emergency vehicles and funeral processions need special clearance and reduced speed. Pedestrians follow signals and crosswalk rules; you yield until they are safe, and you never pass a vehicle stopped for them. Care for pedestrians applies everywhere, with extra caution for children and people with white canes or guide dogs.

## Quiz

1. At a four-way stop, two vehicles arrive from different directions at about the same time. Who yields?
A) The vehicle on the right yields to the vehicle on the left
B) The vehicle on the left yields to the vehicle on the right
C) The larger vehicle goes first
D) Whoever entered the intersection first keeps priority no matter what
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** At all-way stops, same-time arrivals mean left yields to right; entering first does not guarantee right-of-way. *(Lesson 2)*

2. Before entering a highway from a side road with no crosswalk, where must you stop?
A) Only if you see traffic
B) Past the edge of the road so you can see better
C) Before the travelled portion of the highway you are entering
D) At the middle of the intersection
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** With no crosswalk, stop before the travelled portion you are joining, after a full stop and only when safe. *(Lesson 2)*

3. When turning left, you must yield to:
A) No one if your light is green
B) Only vehicles, not pedestrians
C) Any vehicle or pedestrian within or so close to the intersection that a crash could result
D) Traffic behind you only
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** Left turns require yielding to vehicles and pedestrians close enough that a collision could result. *(Lesson 3)*

4. What does a yield sign require?
A) Speed up to merge without looking
B) Slow enough that you can stop if needed, yield to pedestrians and traffic on the roadway, stop if necessary, then proceed with caution
C) A full stop every time in all conditions
D) Only a pause if no one is directly in front of you
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** Yield means be able to stop if needed, yield to those on the roadway, stop when necessary, then proceed with caution (not always a full stop). *(Lesson 7)*

5. When an emergency vehicle approaches with siren and warnings, you should:
A) Speed up to clear the lane
B) Stop in the intersection to watch
C) Move clear of intersections, go to the right curb or edge, stop until it passes (or pull left or right on a one-way street as appropriate)
D) Continue if you are already in a left-turn lane
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** You must clear intersections, move to the curb edge, and remain stopped until emergency vehicles pass (one-way rules apply). *(Lesson 6)*

6. At a flashing amber light, drivers must:
A) Treat it exactly like a red light
B) Enter with caution and yield to pedestrians in crosswalks
C) Never yield because amber only warns drivers
D) Stop once, then never look at the crosswalk again
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** Flashing amber means enter with caution and yield to pedestrians in crosswalks; drivers are not required to full-stop solely because of amber. *(Lesson 8)*

7. A pedestrian is in the crosswalk with "walk" or a green light. When may you drive across their path?
A) As soon as they step into the street
B) When you think you can beat them
C) Only after they are safely across
D) Whenever your light is green regardless of their position
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** With "walk" or green for the pedestrian, you yield and wait until they are safely across. *(Lesson 8)*

8. The vehicle ahead stops at a crosswalk for a pedestrian. You may:
A) Pass slowly on the left if you do not see anyone
B) Pass on the right if the curb lane is open
C) Sound the horn and go around
D) Not pass the stopped vehicle
**Correct Answer: D**
**Why:** Passing a vehicle stopped for a pedestrian is illegal and hides people in the crosswalk. *(Lesson 9)*

9. Who must ensure passengers under 16 wear seat belts when belts are available?
A) Each passenger under 16
B) The front-seat passenger only
C) The driver
D) Only parents, not drivers
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** The driver is responsible for passengers under 16 using available belts and proper child restraints. *(Lesson 1)*

10. Near a funeral procession with headlights on, approaching drivers should:
A) Pass through the line from behind if a gap opens
B) Maintain speed and weave through
C) Reduce speed to half the posted limit and not pass through from behind
D) Ignore the procession unless police are present
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** Approaching drivers slow to half the posted limit and do not pass through a funeral procession from behind; red light or stop requires police direction. *(Lesson 6)*
