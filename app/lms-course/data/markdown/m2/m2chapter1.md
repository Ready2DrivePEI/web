# Traffic Signs, Signals, Pavement Markings, and Work Zones

## Lesson 1: Green, Amber, and Red Lights

### Why Traffic Lights Exist

Traffic lights are placed at busy intersections to make them safer. Both drivers and pedestrians must obey traffic lights, except when a police or traffic officer directs otherwise.

### Green Lights

A green light is not an absolute right-of-way. It is only qualified permission to proceed carefully in the direction the signal allows. It does not let you rush into an intersection without regard for the safety of others.

When the green light is in your favour, you must:

*   **Vehicles and pedestrians already there**: Yield the right-of-way to any other vehicles lawfully within an intersection, and to pedestrians within the crosswalk.
*   **Green arrow with red**: Proceed only in the direction of the green arrow when a green arrow signal is accompanied by a red light.
*   **Turns**: When turning right or left, yield the right-of-way to any pedestrians who are within your half of the roadway, or so close that there may be danger of a collision.
*   **Clearing the intersection**: Do not enter an intersection unless you are certain of clearing it before the next change of signal lights.

### Amber Lights

The purpose of the amber light is to allow people already in the intersection to clear it before the light changes.

Have your car under control, and never speed up as you approach an intersection. Try to notice traffic lights at least a block ahead so you can anticipate a green light changing before you arrive. It is a violation of the Highway Traffic Act to enter an intersection on an amber light, unless stopping cannot be done safely.

### Red Lights

A red light means you must bring your vehicle to a stop at a clearly marked stop line or, if none, at the nearest crosswalk or, if none, before entering the intersection.

Wait until the signal changes to green before proceeding through the intersection, unless signs or signals permit special movements.

> 💡 NOTE: Green still means yield to traffic and pedestrians already in the intersection or crosswalk. Amber is not a signal to speed up.

## Lesson 2: Right Turns on Red and Flashing Lights

### Right Turns at a Red Light

A right turn is permitted at a red light after making a complete stop, if no sign prohibits the turn, and subject to the right-of-way of pedestrians or other traffic in or approaching the intersection.

Do not impede traffic behind you after you have stopped; when the way is clear, proceed safely.

### Flashing Red Light

A flashing red light means you must bring your vehicle to a complete stop and proceed only when it is safe to do so.

The flashing red light may be an overhead light, a standard traffic signal light, or a beacon located directly above a stop sign.

### Flashing Amber Light

A flashing amber light means caution. When facing a flashing amber light, slow down and be prepared to stop.

## Lesson 3: School Signal Lights

### Protecting School Crossings

At many points where traffic is heavy, signal lights have been placed at intersections and between intersections to make crossing safer for children. These signals operate when children are going to or from school. There may be advance warning signs before the signal light.

## Lesson 4: Railway Crossing Signals and Signs

### If You Drive Outside PEI

Although there are no railways in Prince Edward Island, take extra caution when driving outside PEI. Signal lights or warning lights flash when a train is approaching. When approaching a railway crossing where a signal device warns of an approaching train, you must stop not less than five metres (15 feet) from the nearest rail. The same rule applies when a flagperson signals that a train is coming.

Collisions between motor vehicles and trains are among the deadliest. Public highway and railway crossings are marked with signs and may have mechanical or electrical warning devices.

### Advance Warning Signs

These signs tell you to look, listen, and slow down because you may have to stop. There may be an advisory speed tab below the advance warning sign to show that the safe road speed is less than the posted speed. The advance warning sign also advises you of the angle at which the railway crosses the road.

### Pavement Markings at Crossings

Pavement markings may include an "X" and/or a STOP line painted on the pavement at the approach to highway and railway crossings.

### Railway Crossing Signs

Railway crossing signs are found at all public highway and railway crossings. A railway crossing sign means drivers must yield to all trains. If there is more than one track, the sign below the railway crossing sign indicates the number of tracks.

### Stop Sign at the Crossing

If a stop sign is erected at a highway and railway crossing, the driver must stop no closer than five metres (15 feet) and no further than 15 metres (45 metres) from the nearest rail. Drivers must not proceed until they can do so safely.

### Flashing Lights and Bell

If flashing lights and a bell are erected at a highway and railway crossing, the driver must stop when the lights and bell are activated because that means a train is approaching. All drivers must stop at the stop line painted on the roadway or, if none exists, no closer than five metres (15 feet) from the nearest rail. Do not proceed until the lights and bell have stopped, the train has passed, or until you can do so safely. If there is more than one track, make sure all tracks are clear before crossing.

> ⚠️ WARNING: Trains and crossings  
> Never treat a railway crossing like a normal intersection. Stop at the required distance, verify all tracks are clear, and only proceed when it is safe.

## Lesson 5: Crosswalks and Pedestrian Controls

### Crosswalks in General

Crosswalks are provided at intersections and other locations where they may be required by the volume of vehicles and pedestrians.

### Crosswalks at Intersections

Crosswalks connect sidewalks on each side of the roadway and show the path pedestrians should follow when crossing. At intersections without traffic signals, drivers are required to stop when a pedestrian enters the crosswalk on or close to the side of the roadway the driver is using.

### School Crosswalks and Crossing Guards

School crosswalks are provided where the school or community posts a crossing guard to control students and traffic so students have a safe time to cross. When a school crossing guard is posted, drivers must stop when signalled to do so by the crossing guard and remain stopped until the crosswalk is clear and the guard has left the crosswalk.

### Special Crosswalks

Overhead signs and flashing amber beacons give greater warning to drivers, especially on multi-lane streets. When special crosswalks are installed and beacons are activated by pedestrians, drivers must stop before entering the crosswalk when the beacons are flashing.

### Crosswalks With Traffic Signals

Where crosswalks are equipped with traffic signals, the driver must respond to the traffic signals.

## Lesson 6: Yellow Lines, White Lines, and Other Pavement Markings

### What Pavement Markings Do

Pavement markings are placed on a highway to show where the driving lanes are. They can also give other appropriate information.

### Yellow Lines

Yellow lines separate traffic travelling in opposite directions. The yellow line must always be on the left of the driver except when passing another vehicle. Yellow lines also mark the left shoulder on ramps and multi-lane highways. Solid yellow lines indicate that passing is not safe at that location. Broken yellow lines indicate that passing is permitted if there is no opposing traffic.

### White Lines

White lines separate traffic lanes in the same direction and mark the right shoulder line along the roadway. Shoulder lines are solid except at intersections where they turn onto the other roadway or become broken at a ramp. Solid lines indicate that lane changes should not be made. Broken lines indicate that lane changes may occur with proper precautions.

White lane lines at intersections may be wider and shorter with shorter spaces to show that vehicles will be merging, changing lanes, accelerating, and braking to make manoeuvres. Drivers should be alert to traffic changing lanes at these locations.

### Diagonal Markings and Crosshatches

Diagonal markings or crosshatches may appear ahead of traffic islands, merging areas, lane transitions, or medians to warn that the paved area is not intended for use as a traffic lane. Drivers should not use such areas as traffic lanes and should observe the colour of the markings and keep to the appropriate side.

### Stop Lines

Stop lines may be painted at intersections to show where to stop. The driver should stop so that the front of the vehicle does not extend beyond the stop lines. Stop lines are always white. Stop signs do not show where to stop.

### Crosswalk Lines

Crosswalk lines are painted on the highway surface to show where a crosswalk is. When a stop is required and there is a crosswalk without a stop line, a driver must stop before any part of the vehicle extends into the crosswalk. Crosswalk lines are always white.

### Intersection Arrows

Intersection arrows may be used ahead of an intersection to show which lane a driver must use for a specific manoeuvre. Intersection arrows are always white.

## Lesson 7: Sign Shapes, Stop, Yield, and Regulatory Basics

### How Signs Work With Signals

Along with traffic signal lights, traffic signs support safe and efficient movement of traffic. Types include regulatory, warning, construction, and information signs. Shape, colour, and the message on the sign all carry meaning for the driver.

For example, red indicates prohibited manoeuvres while green designates permitted movements. Stop signs always have an eight-sided shape, yield signs are always triangular, and speed limit signs are always a vertical rectangle. Each shape has a distinctive meaning so that if darkness, rain, or snow obscure the lettering, the safety message can still be recognized.

All Canadian provinces and territories use uniform traffic signs. In most instances these match those used in the United States. Learn them so you recognize their message and obey their direction.

### Stop Sign

Stop sign: white letters on red background.

*   Make a complete stop at the clearly marked stop line, or before entering the crosswalk, or where there are no stop lines or crosswalks, before entering the intersection.
*   Look right, left, and ahead and yield to traffic and pedestrians before proceeding.

### Yield Sign

Yield sign: red on white.

*   Slow down when approaching this sign.
*   Look left, right, and ahead and yield to traffic and pedestrians.
*   Stop when necessary to avoid pedestrians or traffic.

### Vertical Rectangle Regulatory Signs

Vertical rectangle signs are white with black or other coloured lettering. These regulatory signs state the law, such as speed limits, parking regulations, and turning and passing movements.

Examples described in the handbook include: traffic allowed in one direction only; a divided highway where approaching vehicles must keep to the right; and a sign on the right facing traffic entering the roadway or ramp in the wrong direction, sometimes with a white on red WRONG WAY sign farther down the prohibited direction.

### Playground Warning Sign

Reduce speed and be prepared to make an emergency stop, as children may run onto the roadway.

## Lesson 8: Construction Zones and Information Signs

### Construction Signs

Traffic can be delayed because of construction or repair operations. Obey the flagperson's signal.

Speed limits in construction zones must be obeyed at all times, even when workers and equipment are not working. Hazards such as bumps, detours, fresh oil, and loose gravel may still be present.

The handbook illustrates construction-related messages such as construction ahead, road work ahead, survey crew ahead, stopping prohibited, lane ends, detour ahead, and bump or rough road ahead, among others.

### Information Signs

Guide signs help motorists along established roads, inform about intersecting roads, and give information in a simple, direct way. These signs are square in shape and indicate the location of various facilities (for example gas, hospital zone, food, lodging, telephone, campground, picnic tables, airport, ferry, Trans-Canada Highway, and PEI highway route in the handbook illustrations).

## Lesson 9: Regulatory, Warning, and Lane Control Signs

### More Regulatory Examples

The handbook shows many regulatory signs, including no left turn, no U-turn, do not enter, no stopping, passing permitted when posted, do not litter, keep to the right of a traffic island, turns permitted as shown, maximum speed limit ahead, truck route, no turns, no right on red signal, school zone, access for the physically challenged, no parking in either direction, one way, snowmobile route, parking, dangerous goods route, cul-de-sac, dangerous goods prohibited, snowmobile restricted, weight limits, and school bus stop ahead.

School bus signs: when amber lights are activated, slow down and be prepared to stop. When red lights are activated and flashing red, all traffic must stop.

### Warning Signs

Warning signs guide drivers around sharp curves, alert them to hidden intersections, merges, steep grades, rough road, narrow road, signals ahead, slippery conditions, trucks entering, bridge issues, fire truck entrances, school bus and snowmobile entrances, bicycle crossing, bridge freezes, and similar hazards. Posted speed on a warning may indicate a safe speed for a turn or curve.

### Lane Arrows and Side-Mounted Lane Designation

Lane arrows show which movements are allowed from a lane, such as left turn only, right turn only, straight through only, or combinations allowed. Side-mounted multiple lane designation signs repeat lane use beside the roadway.

The handbook also illustrates a two-way left-turn lane.

### Change Lane Destination Lights

The sign section illustrates steady and flashing green arrows, steady amber arrow, transit priority signal, steady through-lane indication, and flashing or steady red X used to control which lane may be used.

### Railway and Crosswalk Signs (Illustrated)

The handbook shows the crossbuck, number of tracks, advance posting before a crossbuck, pedestrian crosswalk sign, and school crosswalk sign.

### Additional Signs

Examples include wide load or oversized, pedestrians prohibited, slow moving vehicle, pedestrian pushbutton, cycling prohibited, littering prohibited, and reserved bicycle lane.

## Lesson 10: Using Signals, Signs, and Markings Together

### One System

Signals tell you what to do at that moment. Signs warn and regulate what is ahead or what is allowed. Pavement markings show lane position and stopping points. At the same intersection you may need to read all three at once.

Construction zones add temporary signs, lower speed limits, and flagperson directions that override your usual habits until you leave the zone.

### Summary

Traffic lights give qualified permission on green, require a full stop on red, and limit amber entries to when stopping cannot be done safely. Right on red requires a full stop first and yielding to others. Flashing red means stop and go only when safe; flashing amber means caution. School signals protect children during school travel times. Outside PEI, railway crossings demand stops at set distances and respect for lights, bells, gates, and flagpersons. Crosswalks, guards, beacons, and signals each change when and how you must stop for people on foot. Yellow and white pavement markings separate traffic, show passing rules, stop lines, crosswalks, and lane use. Signs use shape and colour so you can read them even in poor visibility; obey construction zones and information guides the same way.

### Quiz

1. A steady green light means you may enter the intersection without yielding to anyone already lawfully in your path.
A) True
B) False
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** Green is only qualified permission; you must still yield to vehicles lawfully in the intersection and pedestrians in the crosswalk. *(Lesson 1)*

2. You may enter an intersection on an amber light whenever you believe you can clear it before red.
A) True
B) False
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** Entering on amber is a violation unless stopping cannot be done safely. *(Lesson 1)*

3. A right turn on red is allowed when:
A) You slow down without fully stopping if the way looks clear
B) You make a complete stop first, no sign prohibits the turn, and you yield to pedestrians and other traffic
C) You only need to stop if pedestrians are visible
D) You may turn without stopping after 3 seconds
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** Right on red requires a complete stop, no prohibiting sign, and yielding to pedestrians and other traffic. *(Lesson 2)*

4. A flashing red traffic signal means:
A) Slow down but you never need to stop
B) Stop completely and proceed only when safe
C) Treat it like a flashing amber
D) Speed up to clear quickly
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** Flashing red requires a complete stop, then proceed only when safe. *(Lesson 2)*

5. At a school crosswalk with a posted crossing guard, you may proceed when:
A) The students have cleared your half of the road
B) You honk and the guard waves
C) The crosswalk is clear and the guard has left the crosswalk after you were signalled to stop
D) Your light is green
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** You must stop when the guard signals and remain stopped until the crosswalk is clear and the guard has left the crosswalk. *(Lesson 5)*

6. A solid yellow line on your side of the center of the road generally means:
A) Passing is encouraged
B) Passing is not safe at that location
C) You may pass if you flash your headlights
D) The lane is for turning only
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** Solid yellow lines indicate passing is not safe there; broken yellow allows passing if no opposing traffic. *(Lesson 6)*

7. If there is a crosswalk but no stop line, where must you stop when a stop is required?
A) With your front bumper past the crosswalk for a better view
B) Before any part of your vehicle extends into the crosswalk
C) In the crosswalk if no pedestrians are present
D) Only at the sign, not the crosswalk
**Correct Answer: B**
**Why:** When a stop is required and there is a crosswalk without a stop line, stop before any part of the vehicle extends into the crosswalk. *(Lesson 6)*

8. Speed limits in construction zones must be obeyed:
A) Only when workers are present
B) Only during daylight
C) At all times, even when workers and equipment are not working
D) Only if a police officer is present
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** Construction zone speed limits apply at all times; hazards may remain when workers are gone. *(Lesson 8)*

9. The shape of a yield sign is always:
A) Eight-sided
B) Square
C) Triangular
D) Circular
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** Yield signs are always triangular; stop signs are eight-sided. *(Lesson 7)*

10. When special crosswalk beacons are activated by pedestrians, drivers must:
A) Slow slightly but keep moving if the lane is open
B) Stop only if a pedestrian is already in front of your bumper
C) Stop before entering the crosswalk when the beacons are flashing
D) Treat the beacons as decoration
**Correct Answer: C**
**Why:** When beacons are activated, drivers must stop before entering the crosswalk. *(Lesson 5)*
